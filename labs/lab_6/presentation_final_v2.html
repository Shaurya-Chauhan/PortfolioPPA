Predicting High-Risk Buildings in Philadelphia

From Reactive Inspections to Risk-Based Prioritization

Liz Crouse, Shaurya Chauhan, Arzoo Gupta

2026-04-28

The Problem: A Reactive Inspection System

Philadelphia’s building inspection system is driven by complaints, not risk:

  • Only ~7% of rental units inspected annually (Pew Charitable Trusts, 2021)
  • Fewer than 45 inspectors citywide (Pew, 2021)
  • Complaint-driven system → systematic underreporting in vulnerable communities (Urban Institute, 2022)
  • All buildings treated equally despite highly unequal risk

The city lacks a systematic way to prioritize which buildings need inspection most.

Why This Matters: Inspection Results

These results show that only a small share of inspections fail (~5%).

However, these failures are not random—they cluster and follow patterns.

→ Opportunity to prioritize inspections based on risk.

Final Model Predictors and Key Interactions

What we capture

  • Spatial risk
    • Proximity to other inspections
    • Distance to transit and city center
  • Neighborhood context
    • Vacancy rate
    • Income and rent burden
    • Share of renter-occupied housing

Behavioral & environmental signals

  • 311 complaint activity
  • Property characteristics (age, value, density)
  • Certification type (controls for inspection differences)

Key idea

  • Risk is shaped by how these factors interact — not any single variable

Limitations and Bias

The training data reflects enforcement, not true risk
The model learns from where the city already inspects—complaint-driven neighborhoods are overrepresented. It captures enforcement patterns, not underlying building conditions.

Underreporting compounds over time
Vulnerable communities call 311 less → lower predicted risk → fewer inspections → continued under-enforcement. The Pct_Renter_Occupied × log_n_requests interaction captures this partially but does not resolve it.

Certification type dominates (OR = 3.66)
Sprinkler inspections fail at 3.7× the baseline rate. This is a legitimate control variable but means the model is partly predicting what kind of inspection was scheduled, not just building condition.

Residual spatial autocorrelation
Moran’s I on residuals shows remaining spatial structure, suggesting spatial cross-validation and additional spatial features as clear next steps.

This is a decision-support tool. It should inform inspector deployment, not replace human judgment.

Implementation and Safeguards

How the city uses this:

  1. Run final model on all L&I-registered properties periodically
  2. Assign predicted failure probability (0–1) to each building
  3. Sort into risk tiers (High / Medium / Low) by district
  4. Produce prioritized inspection lists for field staff
  5. Retrain quarterly as new inspection outcomes accumulate

Required safeguards:

  • Human oversight: scores inform, not replace, inspector decisions
  • Equity audits by census tract: monitor for systematic over/under-prediction in lower-income areas
  • Transparent methodology: public documentation and grievance process
  • Model monitoring: flag degradation in cross-validated AUC over time
